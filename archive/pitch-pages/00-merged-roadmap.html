From AI Consulting to AI Agent Platform: The Three-Phase Roadmap

A detailed execution plan that merges consulting-led implementation with autonomous AI agent deployment — including exact models, tools, costs, and what needs to happen before the first sales call.
Before You Sell — Pre-Sales Preparation

What must be ready before the first outreach call

You cannot pitch AI workflow transformation if you can't demonstrate it. The following is the minimum viable preparation required before your first client conversation. Target: 2–3 weeks of focused effort for two co-founders working in parallel.

B. Commercial documents — written and reviewed

  • Service one-pager (per vertical): One page. What you do, for whom, typical outcomes, three engagement tiers. This is what you send before the first meeting. Use the 5 pitch pages you already have as the basis.
  • AI Readiness Assessment questionnaire: 20–30 questions covering: current tools and systems, data maturity, workflow pain points, team digital comfort, compliance requirements, budget expectations. Standardised intake — use for every prospect.
  • Master Services Agreement (MSA): Legal template covering IP ownership, data handling, liability, confidentiality, payment terms, termination. Get this reviewed by a lawyer familiar with Chinese commercial law. One-time cost.
  • Statement of Work (SOW) template: Per-engagement document. Scope, deliverables, timeline, acceptance criteria, pricing. Fill-in-the-blanks after each audit.
  • Pricing calculator (internal): Spreadsheet that estimates engagement costs: your labour hours, LLM token costs, platform costs, travel. Ensures you don't underprice.

C. Demo capability — one working prototype per lead vertical

Before your first sales conversation, you need at least one live demo that shows real AI workflow automation on real-ish data. Not slides. A working prototype.

For each of your top 2 verticals, build a 30-minute demo in Dify that does one of these:

The demo is your most important sales asset. If you can show a prospect their actual pain being solved in 30 seconds — using a document or message that looks like what they deal with every day — the conversation shifts from "is this real?" to "when can we start?" Invest the time to make the demo sharp.
From demo to diagnostic prototype: These demos become the starting point for your audit prototypes. When a prospect agrees to a workflow audit, you take the generic demo and customise it with their actual data during the 3-day remote prep phase. The demo opens the door; the audit-specific prototype — built on their real transactions — closes the deal.

D. Domain preparation — know their world before you call

  • 15–20 discovery interviews: Talk to SMB owners, ops managers, sales directors in your target vertical. Not selling — listening. Ask: biggest admin bottleneck, current tools, failed software projects, what ROI they would pay for.
  • Industry vocabulary list: Know the terms your clients use. For export: FOB, CIF, incoterms, BOL, proforma invoice. For construction: VO, BOQ, RFI, retention, LAD. For distribution: SKU, MOQ, UOM, lead time. Speaking their language builds instant credibility.
  • Competitive awareness: Know what tools they've already tried or heard of. Know why those tools didn't fully solve the problem. Position yourself as the missing layer, not a replacement.

Phase 1 — Months 1–12

Consulting-Led AI Workflow Implementation

Generate revenue from week 1. Build domain expertise across 10–20 engagements. Accumulate the workflow IP that makes Phase 2 possible.

What this phase is

You are a high-touch AI workflow consulting firm. You diagnose, design, implement, and manage AI-powered workflows for SMBs in 1–2 specific verticals. Every engagement follows a three-layer model: paid workflow audit & proof-of-concept, paid pilot, paid managed retainer. Revenue starts at the audit — there is no free discovery period.

You are not building a product yet. You are building domain knowledge, delivery playbooks, and client trust — which is the raw material for Phase 2.

Phase 1 engagement structure: the three layers in detail

Layer 1

Workflow Audit & Live Proof-of-Concept (Weeks 1–2 of engagement)

Why clients pay for this: The audit fee is a client filter as much as a revenue source. It proves three things:
  • You're serious — you invest in understanding their problem before proposing solutions (not a vendor doing free demos hoping to upsell)
  • They're serious — a company willing to invest ¥15-30K in understanding their own operations is a company ready to act on the findings (this filters out tyre-kickers)
  • Both sides commit before deeper engagement — this protects everyone's time and sets professional expectations from Day 1

Think of it as a business health check. Every factory inspects their equipment annually. Every business does a financial audit. But nobody has ever audited their operational workflows — the repetitive tasks eating 40% of their team's day. You're offering the first one.

What you do — a concrete day-by-day breakdown:

Days 1–3

Remote Preparation (Before You Visit)

Collect from client: 50 recent transaction samples (inquiries, orders, quality records — whatever applies to the vertical), product catalog, pricing rules, process documentation. Send them a simple checklist; most of this lives in their ERP exports, WeChat history, and email inbox.

You do: Load their data into Dify. Build a working prototype of the primary workflow (e.g., inquiry triage + quotation draft for export manufacturers, or order parsing + confirmation generation for distributors). Configure the AI pipeline with their actual products, their actual pricing rules, their actual templates.

By the time you walk in, you already have something to demonstrate. This is not a generic demo with sample data. This is their data, their products, their workflow — running through a real AI pipeline. This prep work is invisible to the client, but it's what makes Day 4 magical.

Days 4–5

On-Site Audit (At the Client)

Morning, Day 4: Shadow the team for half a day. Watch real work happen. Note timing, friction points, workarounds, tribal knowledge. Map the workflow end to end — not how the manual says it should work, but how it actually works. Inventory their data sources (ERP, Excel, WeChat, email, paper).

Afternoon, Day 4: Take that morning's REAL transactions and run them through your prototype. Show the team: "This inquiry came in at 9:14 AM. Here's what the system produced in 90 seconds. Here's what your team sent at 2:47 PM. Compare." Let them see the before and after side by side, using work they did that same morning.

Day 5: Refine with team feedback. Let 2–3 staff members test on live work alongside their current process. Measure the time difference on each task. Interview the boss about priorities and "what success looks like." Identify the top 3–5 AI opportunities. Score each on business impact (1–5) and implementation feasibility (1–5).

Day 6

Deliverable Assembly (Remote)

Compile all findings, prototype results, time comparisons, and recommendations into the final deliverable package. This is not a vague strategy deck — it's a data-backed operational report with a working system attached.

Key deliverables:

  1. Process map of current workflow with actual time/cost per step — using THEIR numbers from the on-site observation, not industry averages
  2. Waste quantification report — exactly how many hours/¥ are being lost where, based on observed data from the shadowing and time measurements
  3. Working prototype tested on 30–50 of their REAL transactions with before/after time comparisons documented
  4. Prioritised automation roadmap — what to fix first, second, third, with estimated ROI for each opportunity
  5. Custom ROI model using their salary data, transaction volume, and error rates — not a generic calculator, but a model built on their numbers
  6. Go/no-go recommendation with honest assessment — if automation won't help enough, you say so. Your credibility is worth more than one pilot fee.
The prototype is your secret weapon. The single most powerful moment in the audit is when you show the client their own data being processed. Not sample data. Not a generic demo. THEIR Monday morning inbox. THEIR actual purchase order. THEIR real quality report.

When a factory owner watches their 47 morning inquiries get triaged in 3 minutes instead of 3 hours — using their actual products and their actual buyers — the conversation shifts from "is this real?" to "when can we start?"

This is why the 3 days of remote prep matter. You're not showing up to observe. You're showing up to prove.

Risk reversal — why this is safe for the client:

  • The audit fee (¥15-30K) is credited toward the pilot if the client proceeds. So it's not a sunk cost — it's an advance payment on the next phase.
  • If the audit finds the client's workflows aren't suitable for automation (rare, but possible), you tell them honestly. No upsell, no pilot, just the truth and the process map they can use however they want.
  • The prototype stays with the client regardless — they can show it to other vendors, use it internally, or throw it away. It's theirs.
  • Total time commitment from the client's team: ~6 hours across 2 days (shadowing morning + testing afternoon). Not weeks of disruption.

What this costs the client: ¥15,000–30,000 (fixed fee). This covers 3 days of remote preparation + 2 days on-site + deliverable assembly. It also filters out unserious prospects — if a company won't invest ¥15-30K to understand their own operational waste, they're not ready for the pilot.

What you learn: Which workflows are truly automatable, what data actually exists, who the internal champion is, what "success" means to the owner. But you also learn whether this client is worth your time. The audit is a two-way evaluation. You're assessing their data quality, their team's willingness to adopt new tools, and whether the economics justify a pilot. Not every audit leads to a pilot — and that's by design. This knowledge feeds Phase 2 agent design.

Layer 2

Pilot Implementation (Weeks 3–8 of engagement)

What you do: Build the AI workflow in Dify for one specific use case, one department, one KPI owner. Load client data (historical quotes, orders, quality records, SOPs — whatever applies). Configure the AI pipeline: ingestion → processing → draft generation → human review queue. Train the end-user team (hands-on, not manuals). Go live in week 3–4. Monitor daily. Iterate on prompts and rules based on real performance. Measure everything: task completion time, accuracy, adoption rate, user feedback.

What this costs the client: ¥40,000–100,000 (project fee, varies by complexity).

Key deliverables: (1) Working AI workflow deployed and operational, (2) Performance Benchmark Report (before vs. after), (3) User Training Materials, (4) Technical Documentation for handover.

What you learn: Exactly which prompt patterns work, which data formats cause problems, where humans still need to intervene, what exception handling is required. This is the IP that becomes a reusable agent template in Phase 2.

Layer 3

Managed Operations Retainer (Ongoing from week 9+)

What you do: Run QA on AI outputs weekly (sample 5–10% of outputs, check for accuracy and tone). Update templates and rules as products, prices, or processes change. Retrain staff as turnover occurs. Expand to adjacent workflows (e.g., from inquiry triage → quotation drafting → post-order documents). Produce a monthly KPI dashboard: hours saved, error rates, cost impact, adoption metrics.

What this costs the client: ¥10,000–30,000/month (recurring).

Key deliverables: (1) Monthly Performance Dashboard, (2) Workflow Optimisation Recommendations, (3) Updated training materials as needed.

What you learn: How workflows drift over time, what breaks, what new use cases emerge, how to maintain adoption through staff changes. This operational knowledge is the hardest part to replicate — and the most valuable part of your moat.

Phase 2 — Months 9–18

Productised AI Agent Templates

Turn consulting IP into reusable, configurable agent systems. Reduce delivery time per client from 8 weeks to 2 weeks. This is where your co-founder's vision starts to materialise.

What this phase is

After 10–20 consulting engagements, you've seen enough patterns to know that 60–80% of the workflow logic is identical across clients in the same vertical. The AR collections desk for Distributor A looks very similar to the one for Distributor B. The tender extraction pipeline for Contractor A reuses 70% of Contractor B's configuration.

Phase 2 is about extracting those patterns into reusable, configurable AI agent templates — pre-built workflow agents that encode your domain expertise. A new client engagement now starts from a 70% complete agent, with 30% customisation for their specific data, rules, and approvals.

What "productised agents" look like in practice

For each vertical, you build an agent template that contains:

  • ✓
    Pre-built workflow graph: The sequence of AI tasks, decision nodes, human approval gates, and exception handlers — already designed from your Phase 1 experience.
  • ✓
    Prompt library: Tested, optimised prompts for each task type (classification, extraction, summarisation, drafting) — tuned from dozens of real engagements.
  • ✓
    Data ingestion templates: Pre-configured parsers for common document types in that vertical (tender PDFs, purchase orders, invoice formats, QC reports).
  • ✓
    Business rule framework: Configurable rules for pricing validation, approval thresholds, escalation triggers, exception categories — filled in per client during a 1–2 week customisation.
  • ✓
    Human-in-the-loop gates: Pre-defined approval points where humans must review and sign off before the workflow continues. These are non-removable by design.
  • ✓
    KPI dashboard template: Pre-built reporting that shows the client their time saved, error rate, and cost impact — automatically, every month.

Phase 2 is where multi-agent collaboration becomes real

This is where your co-founder's vision of collaborating agents starts to materialise — but with the guardrails you learned in Phase 1:

Sequential handoffs (reliable today): The inquiry triage agent classifies and routes to the quotation agent, which pulls pricing and drafts a quote, which hands to the follow-up agent for scheduling. Each agent has a defined role and clear inputs/outputs.

Overnight batch processing (reliable today): The collections agent runs overnight — scanning all overdue accounts, matching statements, drafting reminders, prioritising the queue. The finance team reviews the output at 8am. No human needed during execution, but human review before any action is sent externally.

What's NOT yet ready: Agents dynamically deciding to involve other agents, agents resolving conflicts with each other, or fully autonomous decision-making on financial or contractual matters. Keep human approval gates on all high-stakes decisions.

Phase 3 — Months 18–36

Platform Scale — The Agent Operating System

Your consulting arm generates domain knowledge. Your agent platform scales it. Each feeds the other. This is where the business becomes defensible and where geographic expansion becomes feasible.

What this phase is

You now have two business arms that reinforce each other:

Consulting arm (high-touch, high-margin): Continues to serve new verticals, complex clients, and custom requirements. Every new engagement generates domain knowledge that feeds the platform. This arm is also responsible for diagnostic, change management, and training — the human-judgment work that agents can't do.

Agent platform (scalable, recurring revenue): A library of vertical-specific agent templates that can be configured and deployed in 1–2 weeks. Clients pay a monthly subscription for the agent platform plus a lower-cost retainer for human oversight and optimisation. This arm scales without linear headcount growth.

The One Rule That Protects Everything

Do not skip Phase 1

The research is unambiguous on this: 42% of AI initiatives failed in 2025. Gartner predicts 40%+ of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by end of 2027. 30% of GenAI projects are abandoned after proof-of-concept. Only 130 of thousands of vendors claiming "agentic AI" are legitimate.

The companies that fail almost always make the same mistake: they build the technology before understanding the workflow. They ship agents before earning trust. They automate before diagnosing.

Phase 1 is not the slow part of the business. It is the part that makes everything else work. The domain knowledge you accumulate, the delivery discipline you build, the client trust you earn, and the IP you create — that is the foundation the agent platform stands on.

Your co-founder's vision of autonomous, collaborating agents that reduce operational cost is the right endgame. The fastest path to that endgame goes through Phase 1, not around it.

The conversation between co-founders should not be "your idea or mine." It should be: "We agree on the destination. The consulting model generates revenue and knowledge from day 1. The agent platform scales that knowledge from month 9 onward. Both are necessary. Neither works alone."
