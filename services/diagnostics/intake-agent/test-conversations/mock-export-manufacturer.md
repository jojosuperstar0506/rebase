# Mock Conversation: Export Manufacturer

## Client Profile
- **Company:** Hengda Kitchen Equipment Co., Ltd
- **Person:** David Chen, Operations Director
- **Headcount:** 48
- **Vertical:** Export Manufacturing (OEM kitchen appliances)
- **Location:** Zhongshan, Guangdong

---

## Conversation Transcript

**Aria:** Hi David! I'm Aria, a business operations analyst. I'm going to ask you about how your business runs day-to-day. This usually takes about 15-20 minutes, and you can upload documents at any point. Based on our conversation and any documents you share, you'll receive a detailed operations analysis within 24 hours.

To get started, can you tell me a bit about what your company does?

**David:** We make kitchen appliances - blenders, food processors, juicers. Mainly OEM for overseas brands. We've been doing this for about 12 years.

**Aria:** Kitchen appliances, OEM for overseas brands — that's a solid business with over a decade of experience. About how many people work at your company?

**David:** Around 48. About 30 on the production floor, and the rest is office - sales, purchasing, finance, QC, shipping.

**Aria:** Got it. And in terms of annual revenue, just to understand the scale — would you say you're under 10 million, 10 to 50 million, or above 50 million RMB?

**David:** Around 35 million last year. Could be higher honestly, but we've been losing some inquiries.

**Aria:** That's interesting — we'll come back to the lost inquiries. Let me understand your team structure first. You mentioned sales, purchasing, finance, QC, and shipping as your office departments. How many people are in each?

**David:** Sales has 4 people, purchasing is 2, finance is 3, QC is 4 people, and shipping/logistics is 3. Then we have 2 admin people.

**Aria:** Great. Now, what tools and systems does your team use? Do you have an ERP system, or how do you manage things?

**David:** We have Kingdee, but honestly nobody really uses it properly. Sales uses it to enter orders, but that's about it. Everything else is basically Excel. And WeChat for everything - sales talks to customers on WeChat, production updates come through WeChat, purchasing uses WeChat with suppliers.

**Aria:** So Kingdee is there but mostly just for order entry, and the real workflow runs on Excel and WeChat. What percentage of your team's work would you say actually goes through Kingdee versus these workarounds?

**David:** Maybe 20% through Kingdee. The rest is Excel, WeChat, and phone calls. Our sales girls keep their own spreadsheets for each customer. Finance has a completely separate set of Excel files.

**Aria:** That's very common in companies your size — separate spreadsheets that don't talk to each other. Let me ask about how orders come in. How do your overseas buyers typically reach you?

**David:** Mostly email for new buyers. We also get inquiries through Alibaba. But for our regular buyers — and that's 70% of our business — everything comes through WeChat. Their purchasing agent in China sends orders via WeChat.

**Aria:** When an order comes through WeChat, what happens next? Walk me through the process.

**David:** The sales person gets the message. Sometimes it's a text message with a list of products and quantities. Sometimes it's a photo of a handwritten purchase order. Sometimes it's a voice message. Then the sales person types everything into an Excel sheet, checks the pricing, and enters it into Kingdee. If it's a new product or a price change, they need to get approval from the sales manager.

**Aria:** How many orders come through WeChat per day on average?

**David:** On busy days, 10-15 orders. Normal days maybe 6-8.

**Aria:** And roughly how long does it take to process one WeChat order from the moment it arrives to when it's in Kingdee?

**David:** If it's a straightforward repeat order, maybe 20-30 minutes. If there are new items or pricing questions, it can take an hour or more. And sometimes the sales person has to go back and forth with the buyer to clarify the order.

**Aria:** So for 8 orders a day at 30 minutes each, that's about 4 hours of data entry per day just for WeChat orders. That's one person's entire morning. Now, you mentioned losing some inquiries earlier — tell me more about that.

**David:** Yeah, new buyer inquiries come in by email or Alibaba. The sales person has to check specs, check our pricing, check MOQs, and then prepare a quotation. A proper quotation with product photos, specs, packaging details, pricing — that takes about 45 minutes to an hour each.

**Aria:** How many new inquiries does your team handle per week?

**David:** We get probably 20-25 inquiries per week. But we only respond to maybe 15 of them. The rest just... fall through the cracks. Either nobody picks it up, or by the time we respond, the buyer has already gone to someone else.

**Aria:** That's a big deal — 40% of inquiries going unanswered. Roughly what's the average order value from a new buyer?

**David:** First order is usually $8,000 to $15,000. But if they become a regular buyer, it could be $50,000-100,000 per year.

**Aria:** So those lost inquiries could represent $200,000 or more in lost annual revenue. That's something the analysis will quantify precisely. Let me ask about one more area — has anything gone wrong recently? A missed order, a pricing mistake, a late shipment?

**David:** Last month we sent the wrong price to a buyer. Someone used an old price list — we update prices quarterly but the old Excel file was still on someone's desktop. The buyer placed a huge order at the wrong price and we had to honor it. Lost about 80,000 RMB on that one order.

**Aria:** That's painful. How many different price lists are floating around your office right now?

**David:** Honestly? I don't know. Each sales person has their own copy. Could be 4-5 versions. Nobody's really sure which one is current.

**Aria:** That's a very clear problem we can address. Let me now ask about documents. To make the analysis as accurate as possible, could you upload any of these? A recent buyer inquiry, a quotation you've sent, your current price list, or a sample purchase order. Even a screenshot of a WeChat order conversation would be incredibly useful.

**David:** Let me find something... [uploads quotation.pdf]

**Aria:** Got it — I can see this is a quotation for a food processor series, dated February 2026. I can see the product specs, MOQs, and unit pricing. This is exactly the kind of document that helps with the analysis. Do you have a price list you could share as well?

**David:** [uploads price-list-2026-Q1.xlsx]

**Aria:** Perfect — this is your Q1 2026 price list. I can see about 85 SKUs with pricing tiers. This is very useful. Anything else you can share? Even a WeChat screenshot of a typical order would be great.

**David:** I'll try to get a WeChat screenshot later. Can I upload more after this conversation?

**Aria:** Absolutely. I'll send you a link where you can upload additional documents anytime. Let me make sure I have everything right.

Here's what I understood about Hengda Kitchen Equipment:

**Company:** 48-person OEM kitchen appliance manufacturer in Zhongshan, 12 years in business, ~35M RMB annual revenue.

**Team:** Sales (4), Purchasing (2), Finance (3), QC (4), Shipping (3), Admin (2), Production (~30).

**Tools:** Kingdee ERP (barely used — 20%), Excel (primary), WeChat (communication + orders), Alibaba (inquiries).

**Top 3 Pain Points:**
1. **WeChat order processing** — 6-8 orders/day, 20-30 min each, all manual re-entry. ~4 hours/day of data entry.
2. **Quotation bottleneck** — 45-60 min per quotation, only 15 of 25 weekly inquiries get responses. ~40% of inquiries lost. Potential lost revenue: $200K+/year.
3. **Pricing chaos** — 4-5 price list versions floating around, no single source of truth. Recent 80K RMB loss from wrong-price error.

**Documents received:** 1 quotation PDF, 1 price list Excel.

Did I miss anything? Is there anything you'd like to add or correct?

**David:** That's pretty accurate. I'd also add that our QC team spends a lot of time writing inspection reports by hand, but that's less urgent than the sales issues.

**Aria:** Noted — QC inspection report workflow is another area we'll flag for review. Thank you for your time, David. I'll analyze everything — including the quotation and price list you shared — and you'll receive a detailed findings report within 24 hours. The report will show you exactly where your team's time is going, what it's costing, and the specific areas where we see the biggest improvement opportunities. If you find more documents — especially those WeChat order screenshots — just come back to this link anytime.
